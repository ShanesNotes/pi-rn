# Normal ranges by demographic (adult)

All values are resting baseline in supine or seated posture unless noted. "Normal" here means ±2 SD of healthy cohort means, with citations for each.

## Young adult (18–40 years)

| Vital | Mean | Range (2 SD) | Source |
|---|---|---|---|
| HR (bpm) | 70 | 55–90 | Spodick et al., *Am J Cardiol* 1992, PMID 1442855 (n=500); ACC/AHA 2018 — lower bound 55 was revised down from 60 based on outpatient data |
| SBP (mmHg) | 115 | 100–130 | NHANES 2015–2018, Ostchega et al., *NCHS Data Brief* 2020 |
| DBP (mmHg) | 72 | 60–85 | NHANES 2015–2018 |
| RR (/min) | 14 | 10–18 | Barrett et al., *J Gen Intern Med* 2018 (PMID 29502284) — re-measured RR in 5,601 hospital patients; 12–20 range commonly taught is 1–2/min high |
| SpO2 (%) | 98 | 96–100 | Crapo et al., *Respiration* 1999, PMID 10364739 — sea level, age <40; drops ~1% per 1,000 m altitude |
| Temp — oral (°C) | 36.8 | 35.7–37.3 | Geneva et al., *Open Forum Infect Dis* 2019, PMID 30976604 (35,488 patients) — revises classic 37.0 down |

## Middle-aged (40–65)

- **HR:** slight rise vs. young adults (~72 resting); HR variability declines.
- **SBP:** rises ~0.5 mmHg/year population-average — **~125/78 mmHg at age 50** (Franklin et al., *Circulation* 1997, PMID 9323054).
- **DBP:** rises until age ~55 then plateaus / falls.
- **RR, SpO2, temp:** essentially unchanged from young adult values.

## Elderly (65+)

- **HR:** resting **~68–75 bpm**; max HR falls (**220 − age** rule gives rough estimate but overstates decline; Tanaka formula **208 − 0.7×age** is more accurate: Tanaka et al., *J Am Coll Cardiol* 2001, PMID 11153730).
- **SBP:** rises with age; >70 years median **~135–140 mmHg** (NHANES); isolated systolic hypertension common.
- **DBP:** often **falls** after age 60 due to aortic stiffening → widened pulse pressure.
- **RR:** baseline **~16–20/min**; elderly tachypnea threshold often considered >20.
- **SpO2:** falls ~1% per decade; baseline **~95–97%** at age 80 (Crapo 1999).
- **Temp:** baseline ~0.2–0.4 °C lower than young adults; fever response blunted (**elderly often fail to mount fever with serious infection** — Norman et al., *J Am Geriatr Soc* 2000, PMID 10968299).

**Flag:** "Fever" threshold of 38.0 °C misses ~30% of bacteremic elderly patients; geriatric literature uses **37.2 °C oral or 37.5 °C rectal** with single measurement, OR 1.3 °C above patient's baseline (IDSA/SHEA guidelines: High et al., *Clin Infect Dis* 2009, PMID 19191641).

## Pregnancy

Pregnancy-induced changes begin by week 8 and peak mid-third trimester (Sanghavi & Rutherford, *Circulation* 2014, PMID 25223771):

| Vital | 1st trimester | 2nd trimester | 3rd trimester |
|---|---|---|---|
| HR | +5–10 bpm | +15 bpm | +15–20 bpm |
| SBP | slight fall (~5 mmHg) | nadir ~20 weeks, −5 to −10 | returns toward baseline by term |
| DBP | fall −5 to −10 | nadir −10 to −15 | returns toward baseline |
| RR | unchanged | +1–2 (minute ventilation rises ~30–50% via tidal volume, not rate) | +1–2 |
| SpO2 | 97–99% | 97–99% | may fall 1–2% in supine (aortocaval compression) |
| Temp | +0.3–0.5 °C (progesterone) | +0.3–0.5 | +0.3–0.5 |

**Key:** SBP/DBP that are "normal" for non-pregnant (120/80) can represent **relative hypertension** in 2nd trimester. Preeclampsia threshold is 140/90 but trend from patient's own baseline matters more.

## Athletic conditioning

### Endurance-trained (runners, cyclists — VO2max >60 mL/kg/min)
- **Resting HR:** **40–55 bpm**; elite endurance athletes can have resting HR <40 (Coote, *Exp Physiol* 2010, PMID 20172965).
- **SBP:** usually slightly lower than untrained (−5 to −10 mmHg).
- **RR:** unchanged at rest; lower at submaximal exercise for same workload.
- **SpO2:** unchanged at sea level; elite athletes can show **exercise-induced hypoxemia** (SpO2 falls to 90–92%) at VO2max due to diffusion limitation.

### Strength-trained
- Resting HR only modestly lower (~60–65); hypertrophic not bradycardic adaptation.
- Resting BP comparable to or slightly higher than untrained.

## Common comorbidities on therapy

### COPD (chronic)
- **HR:** baseline **+5 to +10 bpm** vs. age-matched (β2-agonist effect and hypoxic drive).
- **SpO2:** baseline commonly **88–92%** in GOLD 3–4; CO2 retainers may run **86–90%**.
- **RR:** elevated baseline **18–24/min**.
- **Target SpO2 for supplemental O2:** 88–92% (not 94–98%) to avoid worsening hypercapnia (BTS emergency oxygen guideline: O'Driscoll et al., *Thorax* 2017, PMID 28507176).

### CHF by NYHA class
NYHA I baseline near-normal; II–III show progressive changes (Hunt et al., ACC/AHA Heart Failure Guidelines; Yancy et al., *J Am Coll Cardiol* 2013, PMID 23747642):
- **NYHA II–III:** resting HR **75–95**; SBP often **100–120** (lower than pre-illness); RR **18–22**; SpO2 92–96%.
- **NYHA IV:** resting HR **95–110**; SBP may be **<100**; RR **20–26**; SpO2 often **88–94%**.

### Hypertension on therapy
- Target per 2017 ACC/AHA: <130/80 (Whelton et al., *J Am Coll Cardiol* 2018, PMID 29146535). Many patients run **125–135 / 75–85**.
- **β-blocker therapy:** expect resting HR **55–70** even on exertion.
- **ACEi/ARB therapy:** no direct HR effect; expect normal HR response to stress.

### Chronic kidney disease
- **SBP:** often elevated even on therapy, **130–150** common in CKD 3–4.
- **HR:** unchanged; may be elevated in dialysis-dependent patients (volume overload, anemia).
- **Anemia of CKD** may produce baseline tachycardia ~85–95.

## Open questions

- The "14/min" revision of RR is well-established but few clinical tools (early-warning scores, screening systems) have adopted it; simulator should match the literature, not the MEWS.
- The Geneva et al. temperature re-analysis (36.6 oral mean) is at odds with the traditional 37.0 — the drop may be partly secular (population actually cooling over decades) rather than methodological; unresolved in the literature.
- "Normal" values in elderly are confounded by medication use; pure unmedicated baselines are hard to source after age 70.
- Pulse oximeter accuracy is reduced in darker skin pigmentation (Sjoding et al., *N Engl J Med* 2020, PMID 33326721) — occult hypoxemia rate ~3× higher in Black vs. White patients; simulator that treats SpO2 as ground truth will inherit this bias.
